*Introduction*

About
======
Hello! I'm 茜云 (Qian yun). I'm a graduate student in Human Language Technology at the University of Arizona, graduating in the summer of 2026. My background is in computational linguistics, but the work I actually do now sits somewhere newer: building language-driven automation for sales and revenue teams, the space some people call GTM engineering.

The through-line is straightforward once I trace it. I started with language as an object of study, spent my undergrad at UC Santa Barbara learning how text is structured and processed, and eventually realized that the messiest, most linguistically interesting data in a business is buried inside its CRM, its email, and its outreach workflows. Job titles that mean six different things. Free-text notes that carry intent but no structure. Voice patterns that make one person's writing land while another's falls flat. These are all classical NLP problems dressed in a business suit, and once I saw them that way, I stopped thinking of "AI at work" as a separate track from linguistics. It's the same track.

Background
======
Most of what I do professionally right now is building the pipes that language models sit inside: the extraction, normalization, routing, and evaluation layers that decide whether an AI system actually delivers value in a live business context. My recent PeopleLens internship is documented in depth in the two projects on this site. I'm currently at NeuralSeek building an AI agent that automates ideal-customer-profile discovery for a sales team, again owning the pipeline design end to end. In parallel, I've been at UMB Bank since early 2025, working across operations and engineering to bring a stalled RPA automation project to production for mutual fund reconciliation. And I have a running habit of small independent automation projects that scratch the same itch: a weekly ICP-visitor alerting system built with Warmly, n8n, and the Google API, and a data-collection dashboard for a hundred-plus stocks. The projects I'm proudest of are usually the ones that quietly replace a recurring manual task nobody was going to fix.

The skills that actually matter for this kind of work are less exotic than the marketing implies. It's Python, regex, careful text normalization, the judgment to know when to reach for a language model and when not to, working knowledge of the APIs that hold real business data (HubSpot, Google Workspace, and their cousins), and the patience to trace a problem to its actual root cause instead of the one that's fashionable to blame.




I'm looking for a full-time role after graduation, ideally in GTM engineering, sales automation, or an AI-adjacent product role where the work involves building the GTM system pipelines. 
If your team's building at the intersection of language models and go-to-market, and it sounds like our thinking lines up, let's grab coffee!
